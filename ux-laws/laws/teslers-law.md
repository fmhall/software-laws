# Tesler's Law

"For any system there is a certain amount of complexity which cannot be reduced."

## Takeaways

- Every process has an irreducible core of complexity that must be borne by either the system or the user.
- Wherever possible, lift the burden off users by handling the inherent complexity during design and development.
- Do not design for an idealized, rational user — real people behave inconsistently and rarely take the optimal path.
- Make guidance accessible in context so that new users can succeed regardless of which route they take through the product.

## Overview

Also known as the Law of Conservation of Complexity, Tesler's Law observes that complexity in a process cannot be eliminated, only relocated. Every form, workflow, or computation has a minimum amount of inherent complexity, and design choices determine who absorbs it. Engineers can spend extra time hiding complexity behind sensible defaults, smart inference, and clear affordances — or they can offload it onto millions of users who will collectively waste far more time. Bruce Tognazzini has noted a counterpoint: when applications are simplified, users often respond by attempting more ambitious tasks, so simplification rarely reduces total effort, only redirects it.

## Origins

Larry Tesler formulated the principle while working at Xerox PARC in the mid-1980s, where he focused on making personal computing approachable. His core argument was that engineering effort spent reducing user-visible complexity pays for itself many times over across a product's user base.
