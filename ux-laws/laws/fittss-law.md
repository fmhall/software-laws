# Fitts's Law

"The time to acquire a target is a function of the distance to and size of the target."

## Takeaways

- Touch targets should be large enough for users to accurately select them.
- Touch targets should have ample spacing between them.
- Touch targets should be placed in areas of an interface that allow them to be easily acquired.

## Overview

Fitts's Law describes a fundamental relationship in human motor control: the time to point at or tap a target grows as the distance to it increases and shrinks as the target gets larger. The law captures a speed-accuracy trade-off, where rapid movements aimed at small targets produce more errors. In interface design, it argues for sizing buttons and tap targets generously, placing frequent actions close to where users are already looking or pointing, and avoiding tiny controls that demand precision.

## Origins

In 1954, psychologist Paul Fitts studied the human motor system and demonstrated that the time required to move to a target depends on its distance while relating inversely to its size. His research revealed a speed-accuracy trade-off in which rapid movements toward small targets produced higher error rates. The principle has since become foundational in UX and UI design, shaping conventions like enlarging interactive buttons on touch devices and minimizing the distance between user attention areas and relevant controls.
