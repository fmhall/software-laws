# Law of Common Region

"Elements tend to be perceived into groups if they are sharing an area with a clearly defined boundary."

## Takeaways

- Common region creates clear structure and helps users understand relationships between elements and sections.
- Adding a border around elements is an easy way to create common region.
- Common region can also be created by defining a background behind elements or groups.

## Overview

The Law of Common Region is one of the Gestalt principles of grouping. When elements sit inside a shared boundary, such as a card, panel, or tinted background, the eye reads them as a single unit even when they are otherwise unrelated by color, size, or shape. Designers use this to chunk complex screens into digestible sections: cards on a dashboard, fieldsets in a form, or grouped settings under a single tinted block. A boundary or background can be enough to override other grouping cues like proximity, which makes it a powerful tool for organizing dense interfaces.

## Origins

The Gestalt laws of grouping originate from early twentieth-century psychology research by the Gestalt school, which proposed that humans naturally perceive objects as organized patterns rather than as isolated parts. This tendency was named [Prägnanz](law-of-pragnanz.md), the idea that the mind organizes perception according to specific rules. The classic grouping principles fall into categories including Proximity, Similarity, Continuity, Closure, and Connectedness, with Common Region added as a related principle of shared enclosure.
