# Miller's Law

"The average person can only keep 7 (plus or minus 2) items in their working memory."

## Takeaways

- Don't use the "magical number seven" as a hard rule to justify arbitrary design limits — it describes capacity, not a navigation cap.
- Chunk content into smaller meaningful groups so users can process, understand, and remember it more easily.
- Working-memory capacity varies per person and per situation depending on prior knowledge and context.

## Overview

Miller's Law is best understood as a statement about [chunking](chunking.md). The limit isn't on raw data; it's on the number of independent chunks held in working memory at once. A phone number presented as ten loose digits exceeds capacity, but the same digits presented as three chunks (area code, prefix, line number) fit comfortably. The design implication is to do the chunking for the user — through grouping, spacing, hierarchy, and meaningful labels — rather than to artificially cap how many items a menu can contain.

## Origins

George A. Miller published "The Magical Number Seven, Plus or Minus Two: Some Limits on Our Capacity for Processing Information" in *Psychological Review* in 1956. The paper analyzed information capacity in bits and observed that across many distinct tasks — absolute judgment, immediate memory span — humans converged on a channel capacity of roughly seven items. The result became one of the most widely cited findings in cognitive psychology and a frequent (and frequently misapplied) reference in interface design.
