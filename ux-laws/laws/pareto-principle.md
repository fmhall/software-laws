# Pareto Principle

"The Pareto principle states that, for many events, roughly 80% of the effects come from 20% of the causes."

## Takeaways

- Inputs and outputs are rarely distributed evenly.
- A large group of features, users, or actions often contains only a few meaningful contributors to the outcome.
- Focus the majority of effort on the small number of areas that will bring the largest benefit to the most users.

## Overview

The Pareto Principle is a heuristic for allocating attention. In product work it shows up everywhere: a handful of features drive most of the engagement, a small fraction of bugs cause most of the support tickets, and a few user flows account for the bulk of revenue. Treating every problem as equally important spreads effort thin and dilutes impact. Instead, identify the vital twenty percent and invest disproportionately there. The principle is descriptive rather than prescriptive, so the exact ratio varies, but the lopsided shape is consistent enough to drive decisions.

## Origins

The principle is named for Italian economist Vilfredo Pareto, who in 1896 observed that roughly 80% of Italy's land was owned by 20% of the population. He noticed similar distributions across other domains, and the idea was later generalized by management consultant Joseph Juran into the broader 80/20 rule that is widely applied in business, software, and design today.
