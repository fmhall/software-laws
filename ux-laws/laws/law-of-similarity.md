# Law of Similarity

"The human eye tends to perceive similar elements as a complete picture, shape, or group, even if those elements are separated."

## Takeaways

- Elements that look alike will be read as belonging to the same group.
- Color, shape, size, orientation, and motion all signal that elements share meaning or functionality.
- Make interactive elements like links and navigation visually distinct from regular text so users can tell them apart at a glance.

## Overview

Similarity is a Gestalt grouping principle: when visual attributes match, the brain treats those elements as a set even when they are spread across the page. This is why all primary buttons in a product should share a single style, why links typically share a single color, and why a different color or weight on one item in a list immediately reads as "this one is different."

The flip side matters too. If two elements look the same but behave differently, users will assume they behave the same and be surprised when they don't.

## Origins

The Gestalt laws of grouping were proposed by early-20th-century Gestalt psychologists to explain how the mind perceives organized patterns from individual stimuli, a property they called [Prägnanz](law-of-pragnanz.md). They argued that the mind has an innate disposition to organize stimuli using specific rules. The five canonical categories are Proximity, Similarity, Continuity, Closure, and Connectedness.
