# Selective Attention

"The process of focusing our attention only to a subset of stimuli in an environment — usually those related to our goals."

## Takeaways

- People filter out irrelevant information to stay focused on the task at hand. Designers must guide attention, prevent overload, and help users locate the actions and content that matter.
- Banner Blindness is selective attention in action: users have learned to ignore ads and ad-like styling. Avoid styling important content as ads or co-locating it with promotional units.
- Change Blindness causes significant interface changes to go unnoticed when attention is divided or cues are weak. Audit designs for simultaneous changes that compete for the same attentional resource.

## Overview

Selective attention is the cognitive mechanism that lets people concentrate on stimuli relevant to their current goals while disregarding everything else. Because attention is a limited resource, users cannot process every element on a screen — they sample, filter, and prioritize. Effective interfaces work with this filter rather than against it: they make goal-relevant content salient, reduce visual noise, and avoid forms of styling that the user has been trained to ignore. The principle is closely tied to the [Von Restorff Effect](von-restorff-effect.md), which explains why distinctive elements break through the filter.

## Origins

Selective attention emerged from mid-20th-century cognitive psychology. Donald Broadbent's Filter Theory (1958), E. Colin Cherry's research on the Cocktail Party Effect (1953), Anne Treisman's Attenuation Model (1960), and Deutsch and Deutsch's Late Selection Theory (1963) all contributed to the modern view. Research progressed from auditory tasks in the 1950s to visual tasks through the 1970s, eventually framing attention as a limited, divisible resource.
