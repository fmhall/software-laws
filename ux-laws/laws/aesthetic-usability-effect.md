# Aesthetic-Usability Effect

"Users often perceive aesthetically pleasing design as design that's more usable."

## Takeaways

- An aesthetically pleasing design creates a positive response in people's brains and leads them to believe the design actually works better.
- People are more tolerant of minor usability issues when the design of a product or service is aesthetically pleasing.
- Visually pleasing design can mask usability problems and prevent issues from being discovered during usability testing.

## Overview

The Aesthetic-Usability Effect describes a perceptual bias: when an interface looks good, users assume it works well. That positive halo makes them more forgiving of small frictions and slower to flag genuine problems. The flip side is that polish can disguise broken flows, so teams should be wary of relying on stakeholder reactions to attractive mockups as a substitute for usability testing.

## Origins

The aesthetic-usability effect was first studied in human-computer interaction in 1995. Researchers Masaaki Kurosu and Kaori Kashimura at the Hitachi Design Center tested 26 ATM UI variations with 252 participants, rating each on ease of use and aesthetic appeal. They found a stronger correlation between perceived aesthetic appeal and perceived ease of use than between aesthetic appeal and actual ease of use, concluding that interface aesthetics significantly influence how users evaluate functionality.
