# Goal-Gradient Effect

"The tendency to approach a goal increases with proximity to the goal."

## Takeaways

- The closer users are to completing a task, the faster they work toward reaching it.
- Providing artificial progress toward a goal helps ensure users have the motivation to complete that task.
- Provide a clear indication of progress in order to motivate users to complete tasks.

## Overview

The Goal-Gradient Effect describes a simple motivational pattern: the nearer the finish line looks, the harder people push to reach it. In product design this is why progress bars, step indicators, completed-profile meters, and loyalty punch cards work. Even an artificial head start, like a stamp card that begins with two squares already filled, can increase completion rates by making the goal feel closer than it actually is. The lesson for designers is to make progress visible and to frame remaining effort in terms of how little is left rather than how much has been done.

## Origins

The goal-gradient hypothesis was originally proposed by behaviorist Clark Hull in 1932. Hull's 1934 experiments demonstrated the effect with rats that accelerated their pace as they approached food rewards. The principle has been extensively studied in animal behavior research, and later work in consumer psychology extended it to reward programs and motivational systems where it now informs everything from frequent-buyer cards to onboarding checklists.
