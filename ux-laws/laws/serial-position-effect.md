# Serial Position Effect

"Users have a propensity to best remember the first and last items in a series."

## Takeaways

- Place the least important items in the middle of a list, since users are least likely to retain them in working or long-term memory.
- Position key actions at the far left and right of elements like navigation bars to make them more memorable and easier to find.

## Overview

The serial position effect describes how an item's position in a sequence affects how well it is recalled. It is the combination of two phenomena: the primacy effect, which favors items at the start of a list, and the recency effect, which favors items at the end. Items in the middle suffer because they receive neither the rehearsal advantage of the first items nor the short-term-memory advantage of the last. In interface design, this means that lists, navigation, and content sequences should put the most important items at the edges. Companies including Apple, Electronic Arts, and Nike use this principle when ordering navigation and product elements.

## Origins

The serial position effect was identified by German psychologist Hermann Ebbinghaus in the late 19th century during his pioneering experiments on memory and learning. His self-administered studies of how lists of nonsense syllables were recalled established the primacy and recency effects that the law summarizes.
