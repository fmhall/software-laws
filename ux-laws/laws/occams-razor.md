# Occam's Razor

"Among competing hypotheses that predict equally well, the one with the fewest assumptions should be selected."

## Takeaways

- The best method for reducing complexity is to avoid it in the first place.
- Analyze each element of a design and remove as many as possible without compromising overall function.
- Consider a design complete only when no additional items can be removed.

## Overview

Occam's Razor is a problem-solving principle that favors simplicity. In UX, it argues that designers should strip away unnecessary elements rather than add new ones. Every additional control, label, or visual flourish demands attention from the user, so each element should justify its presence. When two solutions achieve the same outcome, choose the one that introduces fewer moving parts. The result is interfaces that feel obvious rather than cluttered.

## Origins

The principle is attributed to William of Ockham (c. 1287-1347), an English Franciscan friar and scholastic philosopher. The Latin phrase "lex parsimoniae" expresses the same idea as the "law of parsimony." Though Ockham never used the razor metaphor himself, the concept of paring away unnecessary assumptions became closely associated with his work and has since been applied across philosophy, science, and design.
