# Parkinson's Law

"Any task will inflate until all of the available time is spent."

## Takeaways

- Limit task completion time to match user expectations so the work does not expand to fill it.
- Reducing actual duration below the expected duration creates a sense of efficiency and delight.
- Use autofill, smart defaults, and similar accelerants to keep forms and flows from inflating.

## Overview

Parkinson's Law captures a behavioral truth: when users believe a task will take a certain amount of time, their effort tends to stretch to fit that window. In UX this means that long forms feel longer the more fields they have, and checkout flows feel slow when each step takes the time the user expects it to take. The lever for designers is to compress both the actual and the perceived time. Pre-fill known data, infer values where possible, collapse multi-step flows where appropriate, and remove friction at every step. When a task finishes faster than the user expected, the experience feels notably better.

## Origins

Cyril Northcote Parkinson introduced the idea in a 1955 humorous essay published in The Economist, later expanded in his 1958 book Parkinson's Law: The Pursuit of Progress. He drew on his observations of bureaucratic work in the British Civil Service, where staff and timelines seemed to grow regardless of the actual work to be done.
