# Paradox of the Active User

"Users never read manuals but start using the software immediately."

## Takeaways

- Users prioritize completing immediate tasks over spending time reading documentation upfront.
- The paradox persists because long-term efficiency gains require learning that users routinely skip.
- Design guidance should live inside the product itself, surfaced contextually through tooltips, empty states, and inline hints rather than separate manuals.

## Overview

Users are not lazy, they are goal-driven. When they open a new product they want to accomplish something now, not master the tool first. As a result they ignore tutorials, dismiss onboarding flows, and learn by trial and error. Designers who assume users will read instructions will find their carefully written documentation goes untouched. The practical response is to fold guidance into the experience itself: make the interface self-explanatory, surface help where mistakes happen, and let the product teach by doing rather than by reading.

## Origins

Mary Beth Rosson and John Carroll introduced the concept in 1987 through their human-computer interaction research. They observed that new users routinely began using computers without consulting the supplied manuals, encountering errors and obstacles along the way, and yet preferred this trial-and-error approach to formal instruction.
