# Von Restorff Effect

"When multiple similar objects are present, the one that differs from the rest is most likely to be remembered."

## Takeaways

- Make important information and key actions visually distinct from their surroundings.
- Use restraint when emphasizing elements; too many highlights compete with one another and salient items risk being mistaken for ads.
- Do not rely on color alone to signal contrast — users with color vision deficiency or low vision will miss the cue.
- Consider users with motion sensitivity when using animation to convey distinction.

## Overview

Also called the Isolation Effect, the Von Restorff Effect predicts that an item which differs from its neighbors will be recalled more readily than the items that surround it. In interface design, this is the basis for emphasizing primary actions, highlighting selected states, and drawing the eye to critical information. The effect only works when distinction is reserved for what truly matters; over-emphasized interfaces collapse back into uniformity, and the original signal is lost.

## Origins

German psychiatrist and pediatrician Hedwig von Restorff (1906-1962) demonstrated the effect in a 1933 study. Participants who reviewed lists of categorically similar items with one distinctive item interspersed showed substantially better recall for the isolated item.
