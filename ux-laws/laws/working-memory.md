# Working Memory

"A cognitive system that temporarily holds and manipulates information needed to complete tasks."

## Takeaways

- Working memory holds only 4-7 chunks of information at a time, with each chunk fading after 20-30 seconds. Show only what is necessary and relevant to the current task.
- Brains recognize previously seen information far more reliably than they recall new information. Support recognition with cues like visited-link styling, breadcrumbs, and persistent labels.
- The system, not the user, should carry the memory burden. Carry critical information across screens when needed — for example, in comparison tables or summary panels.

## Overview

Working memory is the temporary mental workspace that lets people hold information in mind long enough to act on it. Its capacity is small and its decay is fast, so any interface that forces users to memorize values across screens, retype information they have already seen, or hold long codes in their heads is fighting biology. Designs that respect working memory keep relevant context visible, reduce the number of items the user must track at once, and lean on recognition cues rather than asking users to remember things from scratch.

## Origins

The term was coined by George A. Miller, Eugene Galanter, and Karl H. Pribram in the 1960s as part of early computer-mind analogies. Richard C. Atkinson and Richard M. Shiffrin later folded it into their multi-store model of memory under the label "short-term store." The neural foundations stretch back further: Carlyle Jacobsen and colleagues showed in 1935-1936 that ablation of the prefrontal cortex impaired delayed-response performance, providing some of the earliest evidence for the brain region responsible for working memory.
