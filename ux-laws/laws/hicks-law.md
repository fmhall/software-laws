# Hick's Law

"The time it takes to make a decision increases with the number and complexity of choices."

## Takeaways

- Minimize choices when response times are critical to decrease decision time.
- Break complex tasks into smaller steps in order to decrease [cognitive load](cognitive-load.md).
- Avoid overwhelming users by highlighting recommended options.
- Use progressive onboarding to minimize cognitive load for new users.
- Be careful not to simplify to the point of abstraction.

## Overview

Hick's Law, sometimes called the Hick-Hyman Law, predicts that decision time scales with the logarithm of the number of options. Each additional choice forces the user to read, evaluate, and compare, which slows them down and increases the chance they bail out altogether. In interfaces this argues for ruthless prioritization: surface the most likely action, hide rarely used options behind progressive disclosure, and break long forms or complex tasks into smaller steps. The caveat is to avoid stripping interfaces so bare that users cannot tell what to do at all.

## Origins

Hick's Law is named after British psychologist William Edmund Hick and American psychologist Ray Hyman, whose 1952 research examined how the number of available stimuli affects reaction time. Their experiments showed that as the number of options grows, decision time grows with it because users must spend additional time interpreting and choosing among them.
