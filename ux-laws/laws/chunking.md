# Chunking

"A process by which individual pieces of an information set are broken down and then grouped together in a meaningful whole."

## Takeaways

- Chunking enables users to scan content easily and identify information aligned with their goals, facilitating faster goal achievement.
- Structuring content into visually distinct groups with clear hierarchy aligns information with how people evaluate and process digital content.
- Chunking helps users understand relationships by grouping content into distinctive modules, applying separation rules, and providing hierarchy.

## Overview

Chunking takes advantage of the fact that [working memory](working-memory.md) holds only a small number of items at once. By bundling related content into discrete, scannable units (sections, cards, list groups, formatted phone numbers) designers raise the effective capacity of that limited buffer. Good chunking depends on visual hierarchy, whitespace, and consistent grouping rules so users can locate the chunk they need without re-reading everything around it.

## Origins

The term originates from George A. Miller's 1956 paper "The Magical Number Seven, Plus or Minus Two: Some Limits on Our Capacity for Processing Information." Miller observed that while information theory predicted a fixed "channel capacity" for cognition, short-term memory did not follow that pattern, which led to his insight that people expand capacity by recoding raw items into larger meaningful chunks.
