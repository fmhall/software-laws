# Cognitive Bias

"A systematic error of thinking or rationality in judgment that influence our perception of the world and our decision-making ability."

## Takeaways

- We use mental shortcuts based on past experiences to conserve mental energy and make quick decisions, but these can influence our judgment without our awareness.
- Understanding our intrinsic biases increases the likelihood of identifying them in ourselves and others, serving as protection against faulty reasoning and costly mistakes.
- Confirmation bias exemplifies how we seek, interpret, and recall information confirming our preconceptions, making logical discussion on polarizing issues difficult.

## Overview

Cognitive biases are not random mistakes but consistent patterns: anchoring on the first number we see, weighing losses more heavily than equivalent gains, preferring information that confirms what we already believe. They show up in users (how they read pricing, how they evaluate defaults) and equally in designers and researchers (how they pick test participants, how they interpret results). Naming the bias is the first step to designing around it.

## Origins

Amos Tversky and Daniel Kahneman introduced the concept of cognitive biases in 1972 after observing people's inability to reason intuitively with larger magnitudes. Through replicable experiments they showed that human judgment systematically diverges from rational choice theory, and attributed the difference to heuristics: mental shortcuts that quickly estimate probability under uncertainty but can introduce severe and predictable errors. Their work later won Kahneman the 2002 Nobel Prize in Economics.
