# Cognitive Load

"The amount of mental resources needed to understand and interact with an interface."

## Takeaways

- When incoming information exceeds available mental capacity, users struggle to keep pace; tasks become harder, details slip through, and overwhelm sets in.
- Intrinsic cognitive load encompasses the mental effort required for users to retain goal-relevant information, absorb new content, and maintain focus on objectives.
- Extraneous cognitive load involves mental processing that consumes resources without aiding interface comprehension, such as distracting or superfluous design features.

## Overview

Cognitive load comes in two flavors that matter for design. Intrinsic load is the unavoidable effort tied to the task itself: filing taxes is harder than ordering coffee, no matter how good the UI is. Extraneous load is the effort the interface adds on top: confusing labels, irrelevant content, inconsistent patterns, decorative noise. Designers can rarely reduce intrinsic load, but they can systematically strip away extraneous load through clarity, consistency, progressive disclosure, and removing anything that doesn't serve the user's goal.

## Origins

Cognitive load theory emerged in the late 1980s when educational psychologist John Sweller developed it through problem-solving research, building on George Miller's earlier work on information processing. Sweller showed that instructional design choices can directly raise or lower cognitive load during learning, and formalized the framework in his 1988 paper "Cognitive Load Theory, Learning Difficulty, and Instructional Design." Subsequent researchers developed methods for measuring perceived mental effort as a proxy for load.
