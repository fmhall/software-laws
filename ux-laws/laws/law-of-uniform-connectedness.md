# Law of Uniform Connectedness

"Elements that are visually connected are perceived as more related than elements with no connection."

## Takeaways

- Group related functions by visually connecting them with shared color, lines, frames, or containers.
- A direct connector like a line or arrow can make a relationship between two elements unmistakable.
- Use uniform connectedness to show context and emphasize the relationship between similar items.

## Overview

Uniform Connectedness is considered the strongest of the Gestalt grouping principles: an explicit visual link overrides cues from proximity or similarity. A card with a shared background, a fieldset with a border, or a list with a shared rule between items all use connectedness to declare "these belong together" more forcefully than spacing alone could.

In interfaces, this is the principle behind cards, panels, table rows, and form sections. When you wrap controls in a single container, users stop treating them as independent objects and start treating them as one cohesive unit.

## Origins

The grouping principles originated in Gestalt psychology in the early 20th century. Gestalt psychologists argued that the mind has an innate disposition to perceive stimuli as organized patterns based on a set of rules. The five canonical grouping categories are [Proximity](law-of-proximity.md), [Similarity](law-of-similarity.md), Continuity, Closure, and Connectedness.
