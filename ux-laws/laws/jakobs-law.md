# Jakob's Law

"Users spend most of their time on other sites. This means that users prefer your site to work the same way as all the other sites they already know."

## Takeaways

- Users transfer expectations from familiar products to similar new ones.
- Leveraging existing [mental models](mental-model.md) enables superior user experiences, because users focus on tasks rather than learning new interfaces.
- When implementing changes, minimize disruption by allowing users to continue using the familiar version temporarily.

## Overview

Jakob's Law observes that any given product is only a small fraction of a user's total time online. Their mental model of how a search bar, shopping cart, or settings menu should behave is built up from every other site they have ever used. Fighting that model with a clever but unfamiliar pattern forces users to relearn the basics, which raises cognitive load and erodes trust. Aligning with established conventions lets people apply what they already know and concentrate on the task at hand. When change is necessary, easing users into it, for example by offering an opt-in preview and a way to revert, smooths the transition.

## Origins

The law is attributed to Jakob Nielsen, User Advocate and principal of the Nielsen Norman Group, which he co-founded with Dr. Donald A. Norman, former VP of research at Apple. Nielsen pioneered the discount usability engineering movement and developed several widely used usability evaluation methods, including heuristic evaluation.
