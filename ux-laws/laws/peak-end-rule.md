# Peak-End Rule

"People judge an experience largely based on how they felt at its peak and at its end, rather than the total sum or average of every moment of the experience."

## Takeaways

- Pay close attention to the most intense points and the final moments of the user journey.
- Identify where your product is most helpful, valuable, or entertaining and design to amplify those moments.
- Remember that negative experiences are recalled more vividly than positive ones, so failures near the peak or end leave a disproportionate mark.

## Overview

Memory is selective. When users recall an interaction with a product they do not replay every step, they snapshot the strongest emotional moment and the closing moment, then judge the whole experience by those two points. This means a long, slightly tedious flow that ends on a high note can be remembered fondly, while a smooth flow that fails at the last step can be remembered as broken. Designers should invest where it counts: the moments of greatest delight, frustration, or relief, and the final state the user is left in, whether that is a confirmation screen, a success animation, or a goodbye email.

## Origins

The rule was identified in a 1993 study by Daniel Kahneman, Barbara Fredrickson, Charles Schreiber, and Donald Redelmeier. Participants endured two cold-water immersion trials: a short painful one, and a longer one that ended with a marginally warmer final phase. Despite the longer trial involving more total discomfort, participants chose to repeat it because the memory of the ending was less unpleasant. The researchers concluded that remembered experience is dominated by peak intensity and final state.
