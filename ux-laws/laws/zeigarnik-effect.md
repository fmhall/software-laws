# Zeigarnik Effect

"People remember uncompleted or interrupted tasks better than completed tasks."

## Takeaways

- Invite content discovery by providing clear signifiers that more content exists beyond what is currently visible.
- Show artificial progress toward a goal to increase the user's motivation to finish the task.
- Provide explicit progress indicators so users know how close they are to completion and stay motivated to continue.

## Overview

The Zeigarnik Effect captures a quirk of memory: open loops linger. Tasks that are interrupted or left incomplete maintain a kind of cognitive tension that keeps them mentally accessible, while finished tasks fade from mind. Product designers exploit this by showing partial progress bars, onboarding checklists, and content previews that suggest there is more to do — each open loop pulls the user back to close it. The same effect explains why a half-filled profile or a partially watched series feels harder to abandon than one not yet started.

## Origins

Soviet psychologist and psychiatrist Bluma Wulfovna Zeigarnik (1900-1988) discovered the effect during memory research in the 1920s while affiliated with the Berlin School of experimental psychology and the Vygotsky Circle. Her experiments compared recall of completed versus interrupted tasks and showed that the interrupted ones were remembered far more reliably. She later collaborated with Vygotsky at the Institute of Higher Nervous Activity, founded its Department of Psychology, and received the Lewin Memorial Award in 1983.
