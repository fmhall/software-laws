# Choice Overload

"The tendency for people to get overwhelmed when they are presented with a large number of options, often used interchangeably with the term paradox of choice."

## Takeaways

- Excessive options impair decision-making ability and negatively affect overall user experience perception.
- When comparison is needed, enable side-by-side comparison of related items and options requiring decisions (such as pricing tiers).
- Optimize designs for decision-making by prioritizing displayed content (e.g., featured products) and providing upfront choice-narrowing tools (e.g., search, filtering).

## Overview

Choice Overload is closely related to [Hick's Law](hicks-law.md): as the number of options grows, the cognitive cost of choosing grows with it, and at some point users disengage rather than commit. Long menus, sprawling pricing pages, and undifferentiated catalogs all trigger the effect. Designers can mitigate it by curating defaults, surfacing recommended choices, supporting comparison, and giving users tools (search, filters, categories) to narrow the field before they decide.

## Origins

Alvin Toffler introduced the concept of overchoice in his 1970 book *Future Shock*, observing that extensive variety in options can be detrimental to how people make decisions. The idea was later popularized in psychology by Barry Schwartz under the name "the paradox of choice."
