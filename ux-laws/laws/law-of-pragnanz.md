# Law of Prägnanz

"People will perceive and interpret ambiguous or complex images as the simplest form possible, because it is the interpretation that requires the least cognitive effort of us."

## Takeaways

- The eye gravitates toward simplicity and order in complex shapes because simplicity prevents cognitive overload.
- Research confirms that people process and remember simple figures more reliably than complex ones.
- The visual system simplifies complex shapes by collapsing them into a single, unified shape.

## Overview

Prägnanz (German for "pithiness" or "conciseness") is the umbrella principle behind the rest of the Gestalt grouping laws. Faced with a busy or ambiguous scene, perception resolves it into the most stable, regular, and symmetric arrangement available. In interface design, this means cluttered or irregular layouts get mentally re-shaped into something simpler whether the designer intended it or not, so designs that already align with that simpler form will be understood faster and remembered better.

## Origins

In 1910, psychologist Max Wertheimer noticed that a row of flashing bulbs at a railroad crossing was perceived as a single light moving across space, even though each bulb only switched on and off in place. This observation led to the founding of Gestalt psychology and the principles describing how humans visually organize stimuli into wholes, which later became foundational concepts in graphic and interaction design.
