# Doherty Threshold

"Productivity soars when a computer and its users interact at a pace (<400ms) that ensures that neither has to wait on the other."

## Takeaways

- Provide system feedback within 400ms in order to keep users' attention and increase productivity.
- Use perceived performance to improve response time and reduce the perception of waiting.
- Animation is one way to visually engage people while loading or processing is happening in the background.
- Progress bars help make wait times tolerable, regardless of their accuracy.
- Purposefully adding a delay to a process can actually increase its perceived value and instill a sense of trust, even when the process itself takes much less time.

## Overview

Below the 400ms threshold, interaction feels conversational: the user thinks, the system responds, attention stays on the task. Above it, attention drifts, momentum breaks, and users start second-guessing whether the action registered. Real performance is the cleanest fix, but perceived performance often does the same job: optimistic UI updates, skeleton screens, animated transitions, and progress indicators all keep users engaged across operations that genuinely take longer. The same research suggests a small deliberate delay can sometimes raise trust in processes (like search or analysis) that would otherwise feel suspiciously instant.

## Origins

In 1982, Walter J. Doherty and Ahrvind J. Thadani published research in the *IBM Systems Journal* establishing that computer response time should meet a 400-millisecond threshold rather than the prevailing 2-second standard. When commands returned results within that window, applications were said to cross the threshold and were described as "addicting" to users.
