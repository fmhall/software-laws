# Mental Model

"A compressed model based on what we think we know about a system and how it works."

## Takeaways

- People build working mental models of systems and reuse them in new but similar situations.
- Designs that align with users' existing mental models feel intuitive and let knowledge transfer between products.
- Good UX happens when the design matches user expectations — the reason patterns like product cards, shopping carts, and checkout flows are so reliable is that they match a model users already hold.
- Closing the gap between the designer's model and the user's model requires research: interviews, personas, journey maps, and usability testing.

## Overview

A mental model is a user's internal representation of how something works. It is built from prior experience, cultural conventions, and exposure to similar products, and it lets the user predict what an unfamiliar interface will do before they touch it. When the product behaves the way the model predicts, the experience feels obvious. When it doesn't, the user perceives the product as confusing — even when the design is internally consistent.

Designers also have mental models, but theirs are shaped by deep knowledge of the system itself. The job of UX research is to surface the user's model and either conform to it or, when conformity is impossible, teach the user a new one gradually.

## Origins

Psychologist Kenneth Craik introduced the concept in his 1943 book *The Nature of Explanation*, arguing that people maintain small-scale internal models of external reality and use them to anticipate events, reason about consequences, and explain phenomena. The idea was later carried into human-computer interaction by figures like Donald Norman, whose work on the gulf between a designer's model and a user's model is foundational to modern UX.
